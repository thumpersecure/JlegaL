Three Tracks, One Target

The ambulance pulled up to Goldtex Apartments on May 6, 2026, at 2:47 in the afternoon. Justin Horn was on oxygen by the time they got him inside. Dizziness, cognitive impairment, confusion—the same cluster that had hit before, but worse this time. The portable AC unit in his apartment had been running that morning, blowing air that smelled wrong. Two EMS personnel would later report acute dizziness in the hallway themselves. One exposure, one ambulance, one man in the emergency room of a city hospital because the people who collected his rent had decided thermal-sealed ductwork with foil tape was good enough.

It wasn’t random. The tape had been there since April 6. Nicole Cordial, property manager at Greystar, had watched the fiasco unfold. When Horn told her the unit was making him sick, she didn’t offer to replace it. She didn’t offer to test it, repair it, or investigate. She offered him a choice: accept the poisoning or leave. On the recording—he had the foresight to record—that choice came through clearly. You can hear him telling her he has a doctor’s note. You can hear her not denying it. You can hear her saying if he thinks it’s causing discomfort, the answer is not remediation. The answer is to get out.

So he did. But not because he wanted to. Because a landlord running without a valid rental license, in a building with a central HVAC that had failed nine months earlier and never been fixed, had made that the only viable option. By May 5, he was gone. By May 6, he was in the ambulance. By June 15, his eviction notice says he has ten days left to vacate. By June 12, he’s supposed to walk into a courtroom as the primary witness in the assault case nobody properly investigated in the first place.

The ambulance was the moment everything converged. It was also the moment the institutions that should have acted decided they wouldn’t.

Three stories. One target.

The Building That Poisoned Him

Justin Horn rented Unit 806 at Goldtex Apartments in downtown Philadelphia under the management of Post Goldtex LP, owned by the Pestronk brothers. The building’s central air conditioning system failed in September 2025. Months went by with no fix. Justin asked repeatedly to be transferred to another unit—reasonable requests that management consistently denied without explanation.

By March 2026, Justin asked for a portable AC unit as a temporary solution. Management agreed and installed one on April 1. But five days later, on April 6, they sealed the unit’s hot exhaust hose with FSK tape—a foil-backed adhesive tape that degrades when heated.

That same day, Justin used a thermal camera. The FSK tape was registering temperatures between 102 and 114 degrees Fahrenheit. When tape gets that hot, it breaks down and releases volatile organic compounds—toluene, xylene, styrene, formaldehyde. Toxic fumes, in his apartment, from equipment installed by his landlord.

“I need it. I can’t breathe with the air that’s come in there. I got sick. I have doctor’s notes and you guys know that.” — Justin Horn, April 15, 2026

On April 10, Dr. Mark Fabi documented the airborne contaminant exposure in a formal medical letter. On April 15, Justin went to the leasing office and recorded the conversation. Nicole Cordial didn’t deny his illness. She gave him three options, all of which continued the harm: reconnect the AC, lose cooling, or break his lease and leave.

“Then you’re welcome to break your lease and move out.” — Nicole Cordial, April 15, 2026

On May 6, Justin was transported by ambulance to the ER, treated with oxygen and IV fluids. Two EMS responders reported dizziness in the hallway. On May 13, SERVPRO—a national remediation company—refused the cleanup job, saying the contamination exceeded residential scope.

The building’s rental license had expired February 28, 2026. They had 16 open code violations. They were operating 163 units illegally. And on May 4, their lawyer sent Justin a Notice to Quit, calling him a “defiant trespasser.” The eviction was issued the same day Justin filed a harassment report against Nicole Cordial.

On May 24, three weeks after his ambulance ride, Greystar began post-fire remediation on Floor 8—directly below Unit 806—without notifying Justin. A second acute exposure occurred within minutes.

The Assault Nobody Investigated

In the summer of 2025, Stephen Talley, a fellow resident, directed Justin’s attention to antisemitic Twitter content. Justin reported it to building management three times. On August 22, 2025, at approximately 4:24 PM, Talley assaulted Justin in the Goldtex lobby. The attack lasted eleven minutes. Justin sustained an orbital fracture, broken nose, and broken jaw.

When police arrived, the front desk lied twice: they told the officer that Justin was the aggressor, and that no surveillance footage existed. Both statements were false. Justin, while still injured, went and collected the surveillance footage himself—the footage the building said didn’t exist.

“I’m not interested in a plethora of evidence.” — Corporal Snyder, Badge #8068, Philadelphia Police, May 10, 2026

The case was dismissed at preliminary hearing. It was refiled only after supervisory review, with aggravated assault and ethnic intimidation charges added. Four ADAs have been assigned to Commonwealth v. Talley (CP-51-CR-0000673-2026). None has produced the responding officer’s bodycam footage.

The most significant evidence emerged later: Talley’s own recording. Made on his iPhone 11, with metadata timestamped to August 22, 2025 at 4:24 PM, the 38.9-second video shows Justin with hands raised, palms out—a defensive posture, not an aggressor’s. The defendant filmed his own victim after the assault. The post-assault filming is conduct directly relevant to ethnic intimidation.

Attorney David Bercovitch sent a certified preservation letter to Post Brothers on September 18, 2025, demanding all surveillance footage, incident reports, and internal communications be preserved. A judge also ordered footage preserved at the preliminary hearing. Neither the court order nor the preservation letter has been enforced. No footage has been produced.

The Father Who Funded the Landlord

The Jennifer Horn Family Trust of 1993 holds $350,000 to $390,000. Justin’s father, Dr. Abraham S. Horn, is the sole trustee. His mother, Pauline Parnes, was named co-trustee in the original instrument but has been sidelined.

The disability coercion started in February 2025—six months before the assault, over a year before the poisoning.

“I will be cutting back on all your supplemental monies for everything, since you are giving up the disability.” — Dr. Abraham S. Horn, February 2025

Justin’s response was clear: “You want to punish me for wanting to work? For needing a valid ID? Coercing me to go for disability? That’s over.” His father repeated the threat days later.

In 2022, Dr. Horn removed Justin from the successor trustee list. In 2025, he reduced Justin’s annual distribution cap from 10% to 5%. The financial architecture tightened incrementally, each change reducing Justin’s autonomy.

When the rental license expired on February 28, 2026, Justin wrote to his father: stop paying rent to an unlicensed landlord. His father refused and kept paying—$84,403.23 in trust disbursements, primarily rent to the company that was poisoning his son.

“Why should I jeopardize my credit? For the ‘privilege’ of being your guarantor.” — Dr. Abraham S. Horn, May 1, 2026

On May 1, Justin asked for $10,000 to relocate to safety. His father refused. On May 6, the ambulance came. On May 7, Justin asked if he could come to his parents’ house.

“You do not have my permission to come to my house.” — Dr. Abraham S. Horn, May 7, 2026

Less than 24 hours after his son was transported to the emergency room from chemical exposure, the trustee locked the door.

Three institutional surfaces—building management, the criminal justice system, and the family trust—converge on displacing the same Jewish tenant in the same window, from the same building where he was assaulted, using the same leverage: financial dependence, documented chemical harm, and witness credibility. Each actor arrives at the same conclusion—Justin is the problem—from its own incentives. They do not need to coordinate. The structure does the work.

This was not dysfunction. It was architecture.

He’s displaced now. Unit 806 is a total loss. SERVPRO refused the job. The bodycam still hasn’t been produced. His father is still the trustee, still controlling the trust, still the lease guarantor. The eviction stands. The trial is June 12.

This site exists because when the machinery failed at every level—the landlord, the police, the prosecutors, the inspectors, the judges, the father he trusted—there was no backup. No institution moved. So he documented it himself. Everything is here. The dates, the recordings, the emails, the medical letters, the thermal images, the iMessages, the building management on video choosing not to act. It’s all here because institutions didn’t.
